# Template: Price Reduction Discussion

## When to Use
Seller-only. Used during `active_listing` when data suggests a price adjustment is needed — slow showing activity, feedback patterns pointing to price, days on market trending beyond the area average, or low offers. This is a sensitive conversation and the email should open it, not force it. The actual decision is made on a call or in person.

## Context to Read
- Days on market — how long the property has been listed
- Showing volume and feedback from recent `showing_feedback_summary` entries in the contact_log
- `cma_report.md` — the original suggested list price range; is the current list price at or above the top of that range?
- Comparable active listings and recent sales — what are similar homes doing right now?
- `constraints.tags` — any urgency factors that make a stale listing especially costly (e.g., `relocation`, `must_sell_first`, `lease_expiring` on a simultaneous purchase)
- `timeline` — how much time is left; urgency of the decision

## Subject Line
`A Conversation About Pricing — [Street Address]`

## Required Content (in order)

1. **Acknowledge the situation** — brief and honest. Do not open with "great news." Open with the reality: showing activity has been [slow / feedback has been consistent about price / we're at X days on market with no offers]. One to two sentences.

2. **The data case** — present the evidence for a price adjustment:
   - Days on market vs. area average
   - Showing volume and recurring feedback themes
   - Any recent comparable sales that have changed the market picture since listing
   This should feel like a data briefing, not an opinion.

3. **The suggested adjustment** — state a specific number or range. "I think a reduction to $499K positions us better given where comps are landing." Vague recommendations ("we might want to consider a small reduction") are not helpful. Be specific.

4. **What a reduction does** — one to two sentences. Back on the MLS algorithm's radar, fresh for buyers who passed earlier, competitive with current active inventory.

5. **What happens if we hold** — be honest. Days on market accumulate and buyers use it as leverage. A stale listing invites lower offers. This is not a threat — it is a market reality the seller deserves to understand.

6. **Proposed next step** — ask for a call to discuss. Do not push the seller to decide by email. This is a significant financial decision and it deserves a real conversation. "I'd like to talk through this with you — when are you free this week?"

## Tone Notes
- Lead with data, not opinion. The case for a reduction should come from the market.
- Honest and direct. Sellers who are not given straight feedback end up with stale listings and worse outcomes. Being gentle here does them a disservice.
- Not catastrophizing. The tone should be calm and professional, not alarming.
- The email opens the door. The decision and the full conversation happen on a call.

## Do Not Include
- Soft, hedge-filled language that buries the recommendation ("you might want to think about possibly considering...")
- Pressure to decide via email
- Apologies for delivering the message — this is what good agents do
- A final number without any rationale — always show the data behind the recommendation
