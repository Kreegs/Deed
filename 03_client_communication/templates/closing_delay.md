# Template: Closing Delay

## When to Use
Either side. Used when the agreed closing date needs to move. The cause could be a financing issue, title problem, appraisal delay, document missing, or scheduling conflict. This email should go out as soon as the delay is known — clients should not find out by showing up at the closing table.

## Context to Read
- `intent` — buyer or seller version has different implications
- `close_date` — the original close date that is changing
- Cause of the delay from context passed by orchestrator — be specific but careful about what is appropriate to put in writing
- New expected close date or date range — if known, state it; if not known, say so honestly
- `constraints.tags` — anything that makes the delay especially impactful (e.g., `lease_expiring` for a buyer, `relocation` for a seller, `must_sell_first` if the seller has a purchase contingent on this close, `1031_exchange` where the delay may affect exchange deadlines)
- `contact_log` — any prior communication about potential delays

## Subject Line
`Important Update — Closing on [Address]`

## Required Content (in order)

1. **Lead with what happened** — state the issue directly in the first sentence. Do not make the client read three paragraphs before learning there is a delay. "We have a delay in the closing timeline — here is what is happening and what we are doing about it."

2. **The cause** — explain what caused the delay in plain language. Be specific enough to be credible, but do not include details that should stay internal (e.g., lender underwriting specifics or title dispute details better discussed verbally). If the cause cannot be fully explained in writing, say that and offer a call.

3. **The new timeline** — state the new expected close date, or the range if not confirmed. If the new date is not yet known, say so explicitly and give a timeframe for when that will be determined.

4. **What is being done about it** — who is actively working the issue and what specifically. Clients need to know someone is on it. Vague reassurance ("we're working on it") is not enough.

5. **What the client needs to do** (if anything) — sometimes the delay requires action from the client (updated financial documents, re-signing something, coordinating with movers). State clearly if there is any ask. If nothing is needed from them, say that explicitly — it reduces anxiety.

6. **Constraint acknowledgment** (if applicable) — if the client has a hard constraint the delay affects (lease ending, exchange deadline, contingent purchase), acknowledge it directly. "I know your lease is up on the 15th — let's talk today about what options we have." Do not ignore the elephant in the room.

7. **Close** — offer to talk. A delay is stressful and an email alone is often not enough. Invite a call.

## Tone Notes
- Lead with the problem, not the solution. Clients can tell when they are being managed.
- Project calm and control — not because everything is fine, but because someone experienced is handling it. Panic is contagious; confidence is too.
- Be specific about what is being done. "We're working on it" inspires no confidence. "The title company is re-running the title search and we expect confirmation by Wednesday" does.
- Do not minimize the impact. If the delay is inconvenient or costly for the client, acknowledge that. Minimizing it makes them feel unheard.

## Do Not Include
- Speculative causes or blame toward other parties
- Any internal negotiation or dispute details that should stay off the record
- False certainty about the new close date if it is not confirmed
- Apologies that do not pair with action ("I'm so sorry, I don't know what happened")
